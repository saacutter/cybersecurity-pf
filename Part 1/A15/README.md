## Incident 1 - Various Australian Universities
In the past few months, there have been several cyberattacks on Australian university systems (and the higher education sector in general). These include the following:
- [Western Sydney University (2025)](https://www.westernsydney.edu.au/news/cyber-details)
- [The University of Sydney (December 2025)](https://www.sydney.edu.au/news-opinion/news/2025/12/18/notification-of-cyber-and-data-breach.html)

In 2025, Western Sydney University disclosed on several occasions that they had experienced some kind of cyber attack. The first of these incidents occurred in early April 2025, in which it became victim to two separate incidents. The first incident involved unauthorised access to the University's systems gained through an SSO system and involved the theft of data relating to about 10,000 current and former students' demographic, enrolment, and progression information. The second incident involved the posting of "personal information belonging to the University community" (I couldn't find exactly what this meant) to the dark web from a previous cyber incident the year prior. The University responded to this incident by removing access of their systems to the attacker as soon as they detected it, notifying the relevant authorities in response to the post on the dark web, contacting impacted students, and various other protective measures like password resets and deploying more monitoring/detection tools. In June 2025, the attacker allegedly responsible for this attack (and other similar attacks dating back to 2021) was charged with 20 offences. They experienced another cyber incident in early October 2025, where students (and graduates) were being sent fraudulent emails claiming to be from the University. The emails being sent varied in content, with some claiming that the recipient was expelled from the University and others claiming that their qualifications had been revoked. After some investigation, they confirmed the most recent breach three weeks later. In this breach, the personal information of students (including contact information, country of birth, tax file numbers, bank account details, driver's licence details, visa information, health information, and a whole lot more) was confirmed to have been exfiltrated from unauthorised entry into third-party systems used by the University. In response to this incident, individuals were notified (in some cases in regards to several previous incidents) and people were told to change their passwords to accounts. This response, notably, does not refer to upgrading their systems to prevent these kind of attacks (which was [exploited only two weeks earlier using a vulnerability that had been known to the University for years at that point](https://www.reddit.com/r/sydney/comments/1nzezp2/just_got_this_mass_email_from_western_sydney/) and was unaddressed entirely by them). I could not find any information from any source (including the media) on what actions were taken, but if previous history is anything to go by then there probably weren't any taken (or at the very least not any worthwhile preventative measures).

In late December 2025, the University of Sydney also confirmed its own cyber incident. The University detected unauthorised activity in one of their online code libraries (primarily used for code storage and development) on an old system, which also stored historical data files. These data files contained the personal information of approximately 10000 currently employed staff, approximately 12500 former staff, 5000 students, and 6 donors as of September 4 2018. This information included names, dates of birth, phone numbers, home addresses, and some basic job information like job title and employment dates. In response to this incident, the University blocked access to the code library, removed the datasets from the library, contacted the impacted individuals, and monitored the dark web to ensure the data wasn't published.

## Incident 2 - [Qantas Cyber Incident (June-October 2025)](https://www.qantas.com/au/en/support/information-for-customers-on-cyber-incident.html)
In early July 2025, Qantas Australia confirmed that they had suffered a data breach. The media release they published at the time of the incident stated that approximately 5.7 million of their customers had their data held in the accessed system, including their name, email addresses, frequent flyer details, home address, date of birth, phone numbers, gender, and meal preferences (with the number of entries holding each attribute varying). They claimed that in late June 2025 that they had detected unusual activity on a third-party platform used by Qantas. In mid October 2025, they confirmed that the data was released onto the Dark Web by the attackers.

I could not find any information from Qantas regarding the attack itself, but there were various details uncovered by the media. The ABC reported that the attack was performed by the cybercrime group Scattered Lapsus$ Hunters, who are a group which have attacked several high-profile companies in the last year with the primary motivation of money. In this same article, it is reported that the data was accessed by using social engineering techniques to trick a Qantas call centre worker into handing over access to the data and that the third-party platform used by Qantas was Salesforce (which Google's Threat Intelligence Group also reported, and although they use a different name for the attackers they were reportedly working together). Another ABC article claims that the group claimed that the attack was done because they didn't agree with some of the policies of the Australian government, and that Qantas was one of 40 businesses attacked by this particular cybercrime group. It is also reported that the data was released in October because the deadline for the ransom had passed and Qantas had not paid it.

## Incident 3 - [Discord](https://discord.com/press-releases/update-on-security-incident-involving-third-party-customer-service)
In early October 2025, the popular social media platform Discord released a statement confirming that they (well, a third-party provider they were using) had become victims to a cyberattack. The third-party breached was 5CA, a Netherlands-based organisation used to support Discord's customer service. Although Discord did not disclose the exact attack vector, it is reported that it may have occurred through the compromise of a support agent's account at 5CA (even if 5CA suggested the contrary). It is reported that this attack was performed by the cybercrime group Scattered Lapsus$ Hunters, the same group behind the Qantas data leak, as an attempt to get a ransom (which Discord did not pay). According to Discord, approximately 70000 users had their personal information exposed which may have included names, usernames, email addresses, some billing information, IP addresses, messages with customer service agents, and government-ID images. This is disputed by some sources, who instead claim that 1.5TB of data was stolen including over 2 million government-ID images impacting over 5 million users (though Discord has rejected this, stating that it was an extortion attempt).

In response to this incident, Discord revoked 5CA's access to their ticketing system, notified the relevant authorities, and contacted impacted individuals. They also claim to have reviewed their own internal threat detection systems and third-party support provider controls, but did not go into detail on what was reviewed.

## Incident 4 - [The State Government of Victoria Department of Education](https://www.vic.gov.au/cyber-incident-impacting-victorian-government-schools)
In mid January 2026, The Victorian Department of Education disclosed that they detected unauthorised access to one of their databases. This database held information for both current and past students at Victorian government schools, and contained data like student names, school email addresses (and their associated password hashes), school name, and year level with no further information identified to have been accessed. They did, however, find no evidence of this data being released publicly or shared. The access point was identified to have been through a school's network, according to the ABC. It has been further reported that the attacker exploited a single point of weakness and located the database, identifying that there was a structural failure in how the system was designed.

The Department of Education responded by deactivating all passwords for issued email addresses with schools issuing new passwords at the start of the school year (which was scheduled to start in about two weeks after this incident occurred) and the parents/carers of all children impacted were (supposedly) contacted. Additionally, they deactivated any accounts owned by past students. They also claim to have put additional protections in place to prevent such an incident from occurring again, but do not disclose any details on what these protections actually are.

## Incident 5 - [Notepad++](https://notepad-plus-plus.org/news/hijacked-incident-info-update/)
In early February 2026, the developers behind the Notepad++ software released a statement which confirmed that they had suffered a cyber incident. Notepad++, a FOSS application, is a popular alternative to the text editors available on Windows. This incident saw the attackers compromising the infrastructure of the application, intercepting and redirecting update traffic on their domain (https://notepad-plus-plus.org) to retrieve malicious files instead as Notepad++ did not verify the integrity of downloaded files. According to the statement, a third-party hosting server which was being used to deliver updates was compromised from June 2025 until September 2 2025 (when scheduled maintenance took place), with the attackers maintaining credentials to the servers until December 2 2025 (allowing them to keep redirecting update traffic). Researchers confirmed that most users were never affected by this attack, as victims were filtered based on some sort of strategic value. Rapid7 did an investigation into this incident, and found that the attack was executed by the cybercrime group Lotus Blossom. This attack worked by downloading a malicious update file for the Notepad++ software which included extra instructions, delivering a payload that ran a background process allowing a compromised device to be controlled remotely.

In response to this incident, the Notepad++ website was migrated to a new hosting provider with stronger security practices to prevent this specific attack from occurring. Notepad++ itself was also updated to v8.8.9, which adjusted how the updater verified update files (to ensure they weren't compromised again). This incident was published as [CVE-2025-15556](https://www.cve.org/CVERecord?id=CVE-2025-15556), which exploited CWE-494.
